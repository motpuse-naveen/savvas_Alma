Alma and How She Got Her Name

Author: Juana Martinez-Neal

Read about a young girl who learns the story of her very long name!

“Like artifacts, the names that parents give children often have stories to tell. Every piece of Alma’s name, she discovers, comes to her from someone in her family, and, as she and her father talk, Alma feels a new sense of connection…”
— The Wall Street Journal

Ezra Jack Keats Writer Award Honoree 2019

Mirrors & Windows

Inside/Outside Circle

Use the activity below to help students learn more about each other’s names.

Divide students into two equal groups. The first group forms a circle facing outward (the inside circle). The second group forms a circle facing inward and across from a member of the inner circle to make partners (the outside circle).

Tell students you will ask several questions. They will share their responses with their partner. The inner circle partner will share their responses first, followed by the outer circle partner. Be sure students know that it is okay if they don’t know the answers to some of the questions.

After all the questions have been asked, partners thank one another for sharing their responses. The inner circle will take a step to the left and discuss the same questions with a new partner.

Repeat for as long as time allows or until children have rotated once around the circle.

Prompts Choose 3—4 questions from the following list or other questions related to names.

  •   1. What is your name?
  •   2. Who gave you your name?
  •   3. Were you named after someone? If so, who?
  •   4. Do you have a nickname? Who calls you by your nickname?
  •   5. What name do you like to be called?
  •   6. How do you feel when someone does not say your name correctly?

Author’s Craft

Repetition

Tell students that writers choose their words very carefully in order to make their ideas clear and interesting to read. One way they do this is to repeat words or phrases. Explain that this is called repetition. Explain that writers use repetition for many reasons including, to create rhythm, to establish a certain mood or feeling, or to emphasize an important idea.

Remind students of “The Names Song.” Point out that the lyric, “I have a name and so do you. My name is special and yours is, too” repeats throughout the song. Ask: Why do you think this lyric is repeated? What idea does it help you remember? (That everyone has a name and that all names are special)

Tell students that as they read this story, they will look for words and phrases that are repeated and explore why the author chose to repeat these words.

First Read Aloud: Alma and How She Got Her Name

1. Before reading, hold up Alma and How She Got Her Name and introduce the author and illustrator, Juana Martinez-Neal. Tell students this story is about a girl who has a very long name.

2. During reading, display the book so that everyone can see the illustrations. Periodically, stop and ask questions, using a think-pair-share routine, to monitor comprehension, and to promote discussion and engagement. Have pairs discuss each question using these sentence frames: I notice ____ . I think ____ . I wonder ____ . Model the routine as needed using a think-aloud.

  • What do you notice, think, and wonder about Alma’s name? (I notice that she has many names; I think it might be hard to have so many names; I wonder why she doesn’t like her long name.)
  • What do you notice, think, and wonder about what Alma learns about her names? (I notice that each of her names is also the name of someone in her family; I think Alma is surprised to learn about her name; I wonder if she will feel differently about her name.)
  • What do you notice, think, and wonder about Alma’s feelings about her name after she learns about it? (I notice she feels proud of her name; I think it makes her feel special to be connected to her ancestors; I wonder what kind of story she will tell about the name Alma.)

Mirrors & Windows

Tell students to think about how Alma felt about her name at the beginning of the story and at the end of the story. Ask: How do Alma’s feelings about her name change? What does Alma like about her name?

After discussing, tell students that just like Alma, their names are important too. Explain that they will do an activity to explore what they like about their classmates’ names.

Have students sit in a circle. Give a beanbag or other soft object to a student. Ask the student to say his or her name. Repeat the student’s name and tell the student why you like it. For example, “Yoshi, I like your name because it is the same name as my grandfather’s name.” Then, invite volunteers to say something they like about that students’ name. Have them use the following sentence frame:
______ , I like your name because ______ .

After a few students share, have the student holding the beanbag toss it to another student and repeat the process. Continue, until all students have had a chance to hold the beanbag.

* Before starting, remind students that it is important to pronounce names correctly. Tell students that it is okay to ask someone how to pronounce his or her name if you aren’t sure how to say it. You may also want to provide some student exemplars.

Student Exemplars:

“Layla, I like your name because it sounds like music when you say it.”

“Milo, I like your name because it is the same as my name!”

“Rose, I like your name because it makes me think of a flower.”

Second Read Aloud

1. Before reading, hold up Alma and How She Got Her Name and remind students that they have already read this book. Invite students to say Alma’s names with you. Then, display a five-column chart with one of Alma’s names at the top of each column.

2. During reading, add words, phrases, or pictures to each column of the chart that help explain the story of Alma's name. To elicit ideas, ask students questions about what Alma learns about her ancestors and how that makes her feel connected to them, such as:

  • What does Alma learn about (Sofia/Esperanza/José/Pura/Candela)?
  • What word, phrase, or picture can we add to the chart to show this?
  • What does Alma realize about each person she was named after?

3. Review the completed chart with students and display it throughout the remainder of the lesson. Ask: What story does this tell us about Alma’s family? (That she shares some of their interests and dreams; that her relatives and ancestors are a part of who she is)

Author’s Craft

Repetition

Remind students that authors repeat words and phrases for many reasons. One reason is to emphasize important ideas that the author wants the reader to remember. Ask students if they noticed a word or phrase that the author repeated in Alma and How She Got Her Name.

Elicit responses by paging through the book and pointing out the phrase “I am…” throughout the story. Have students read the phrase with you and count how many times the phrase is repeated. Then ask:

  • When does the author repeat this phrase? (after Alma learns about each of her ancestors)
  • What does repeating this phrase help you understand? (that Alma makes a connection between herself and each family member she is named after)

 

Third Read Aloud

1. Before reading, hold up the book and ask students to read aloud the title with you. Remind students that when they learn the story of Alma’s name, they also learn about things that are important to her family. Review the chart they created during the second read aloud to remind students what Alma learns about each of her ancestors. Tell students that now they will think more about the name Alma.

2. During reading, reread the first three pages and the last two pages. Guide students to understand the significance of the name Alma.

  • Why is the name Alma important? (Because it is the part of her name that does not yet have a story. The name Alma will have a story that is just about her.)
  • Review that at the beginning of the story, Alma says she does not like her name because it “never fits.” At the end of the story, Alma says her name “fits her just right.” Point out that fits has more than one meaning, and discuss the meaning of fits in each sentence.

Ask: What does Alma mean when she says her name “fits her just right?” (That it connects her to her family and stands for who she is)

Title: Alma and How She Got Her Name

Author: Juana Martinez-Neal

Genre: Realistic Fiction

Lexile: 490L

Page Count: 32

Publisher: Candlewick Press

Copyright: 2018

 

Plot Overview

Alma Sofia Esperanza José Pura Candela thinks her name is too long. She tells her father it is so long, it never fits on a page. So, Alma’s father tells Alma the story behind her name.

Alma learns that each of her names represents someone in her family. Sofia was her grandmother, Esperanza was her great-grandmother, José was her grandfather, Pura was her great-aunt, and Candela was her other grandmother. As she learns the story of her name, she discovers various interests and attributes that she shares with each family member—a love of books, flowers, drawing, a sense of adventure, protection, and courage.

In the end, Alma learns that her father picked the name Alma just for her so that she could make her own story. Once Alma learns this, she realizes her name is a perfect fit, and she becomes very proud of her very long name.

Theme Overview

Family Stories Family stories connect us to our history. Alma learns the story of her family by learning the story of her name. As her father tells her about the relatives she was named after, Alma gains a deeper understanding of and connection to the people who lived before her. Learning this history makes her feel proud of both her name and her family.

Family stories can also be shared through photographs, recipes, or other traditions that are passed from one generation to another. These stories help us to learn more about who we are and contribute to the development of our own unique identities.

Sensitivity Issues

Before beginning the lesson, be sensitive to your students’ family situations, for example students in adoptive or foster families, or students who may not know specific details regarding their names. Modify activities as needed to support all students in participating. For example, if a student does not want to talk about their own name, suggest other names they can discuss such as the name of a pet, local sports team, or school mascot.

Activity Support

Four Corners: What Makes Me Proud?

The Four Corners activity asks students to reflect on different examples of things that make people feel proud. Use this activity as a warm-up for exploring ideas of pride in The Proudest Blue.

1. Post one of the four examples in each corner of the room. Include a visual for each example.

  • Helping to make a special food for a family or community celebration
  • Working hard to create a beautiful drawing in art class
  • Watching a friend or family member play well in a sport
  • Supporting a friend who is being picked on by bullies

2. Read aloud each example and tell students to think about how it reflects (or does not reflect) things that make them feel proud. Have students discuss each example with a partner. Provide the following sentence frame to support discussion:
   This would / would not make me feel proud because ________ .

3. After discussing each example in pairs, tell students to move to the corner of the room displaying the example that would make them feel the proudest. Remind students that “proudest” means “the most proud.”

4. Give students time to discuss the example they chose in groups. Encourage students to share similar situations that made them proud. Provide the following sentence starters to support discussion:

  This example would make me feel the proudest because ___________ .

  It reminds me of a time in my life when ___________ .

5. Have a student from each group summarize their group’s discussion. Then guide students in a whole-class discussion.

Activity Support

Point of View

Write “Point of View” on chart paper and explain that this refers to who is telling the story. Explain that the person who tells a story is called the narrator. The story is told from the narrator’s point of view—the narrator’s thoughts and feelings about events and other characters. Clues in the text such as pronouns I, me, and we help you identify the narrator. List the boldfaced terms on the chart paper.

1. Read aloud the opening two pages of the story. Ask students to identify the three main characters—Asiya, Faizah, and Mama. Then have students think about the following questions:

  •   a. Which character is telling the story?
  •   b. What clues in the text or the illustrations help you know which character is telling the story?

2. Once students have had time to think, have them turn and talk about their ideas with a partner. After a few minutes, have pairs of students share their ideas with the whole class.

3. Summarize the following points, adding the story details to the chart paper:

  • Faizah is the narrator.
  • The story is told from Faizah’s point of view—it tells her thoughts and feelings about the events and other characters.

4. Clues in the text such as “I know why,” “I know it,” and “We’re sisters” help readers know that Faizah is the narrator.

5. Display the chart paper as an anchor chart throughout the lesson and have students refer and add to it as they read.

6. Finally, tell students to think about why the author chose to make the younger sister the narrator as they read the story.

Activity Support

Responding to Bullies

Begin the activity by having students recall how Milton and his friends in the Stop Bullying Now video dealt with bullying. Then have them recall what Ibtihaj Muhammad had to say about her own experience with bullying.

You might share situations in your life where your own pride or sense of self helped you overcome a difficult situation. For example, “When I was in fifth grade, classmates made fun of me because they thought my singing sounded funny. I ignored them and just sang louder and prouder!”

1.Now pose this question: Why might feeling proud of who you are help you deal with a problem like bullying?

2. Have students think about this question on their own, and then turn and talk to discuss it with a partner. Then, have partners discuss the question with the whole class. (Note that some students may not feel comfortable sharing experiences with bullying. In that case, they can discuss the question as it relates to the two videos that they viewed.)

3. Prompt the whole class discussion with guiding questions such as the following:

  • How does Faizah feel when she first hears the bully laughing at Asiya? Why? (Faizah doesn’t understand it; she asks why; she can’t understand why others don’t see Asiya’s hijab the same way she does—proud and beautiful.)
  • How are Faizah’s reactions to the bullying different from Asiya’s? (Asiya and her friends calmly go back to playing tag; Faizah gets mad)
  • What might Faizah learn from Asiya about dealing with bullies? How does feeling proud help both girls? (Faizah learns to stay proud of herself and ignore the bullies. This helps both Asiya and Faizah let go of the hurtful words.)
  • Have you ever had to overcome a big challenge like this? How did it make you feel about yourself?

 

Activity Support

I’ll Always Be Here

Ask students to think about a lesson Faizah learns from Asiya about being strong and proud of who she is.

1. Have students write a sentence to describe the lesson Faizah learns.

2. Then have students turn and talk with a partner to compare their ideas.

3. Finally, have partners come together with the whole class to discuss the lesson Faizah learns. Use the guiding questions to support ideas and discussion.

  • What does Faizah mean when she says that Asiya’s hijab says to her “I’ll always be here”? (The hijab reassures Faizah; it helps her feel proud and strong of who she and Asiya are—sisters who will always be there to help each other.)
  • How does Asiya show Faizah that she will always be here for Faizah? How does that make Faizah feel? (Asiya walks Faizah home, holding her hand; she looks out for Faizah; she is a good example for Faizah, too)

  • How can you show a friend or a family member that you will “always be here”?

 

Activity Support

Figurative Language: Simile, Metaphor, and Personification

On the chart-paper, display the sample sentences in a table such as the following. Underline the words “like,” “tiger,” and “yelled” as shown. Leave extra space in each row below the examples to show where students will write additional examples from the text as they read the story.

Simile
  • Josh jumped at the ball like a tiger.
Metaphor
  • Josh was a tiger on the soccer field.
Personification
  • Josh’s alarm clock yelled at him to wake up.

1. Have students think about the example sentences, and then turn and talk about them with a partner. Use the following questions to prompt discussion.

  •   What images come to mind when you read these sentences?
  •   What do the descriptions make you wonder about Josh and his alarm clock?
  •   What kind of comparisons do these examples make?

2. After pairs discuss their ideas, facilitate a whole class discussion. Use the examples in the table to review the following key ideas:

  • Figurative language uses comparisons to describe something.
  • Similes use the words “like” or “as”; metaphors and personification do not.
  • Similes and metaphors compare things that have similar qualities.
  • Personification gives qualities of people to things that are not people.

3. Point out that in figurative language the meaning is not literal (Josh is not really a tiger). Figurative language compares two or more things because they share similar qualities (Josh plays sports with the quickness of a tiger).

4. Display the table on the chart-paper as an anchor chart throughout the lesson and have students refer and add to it as they read.

5. Explain that Ibtihaj Muhammad uses a number of these types of figurative language throughout The Proudest Blue and that students can record these examples in their own charts as they read the story.

Activity Support

Unlock a Key Sentence

Display and read aloud the key sentence. Then guide students in unlocking its meaning by discussing each sentence chunk using the protocol described below.

Start with the sentence chunk that tells who or what the sentence is mostly about. Continue to unpack meaning and connect ideas, chunk by chunk, using the guiding questions in the table below.

  “You will | make your own story.”

Sentence Chunk Discuss Meaning
You will - Who is the sentence about? (you)
- Who is “you”? (Alma)
- Who is speaking? (Alma’s father)
- What does the word will tell us? (Alma will do something it in the future)
make your own story - What will Alma do? (she will create a story)
- What does “your own” mean? (that something is yours, no one else has it)
- What does story mean in this sentence? (It means the things that happen in someone’s life that are important; it is a description of what a person is like.)
- What is Alma’s father trying to tell her to do? (to be herself, to do things she likes to do, to let others know what is important to her, to make her own story by being herself)

After unlocking the key sentence, have students craft their own sentence using the following sentence frame:

I will make my own story by __________ .

Activity Support

Unlock a Key Sentence

Display and read aloud the key sentence. Then guide students in unlocking its meaning by discussing each sentence chunk using the protocol described below.

Start with the sentence chunk that tells who or what the sentence is mostly about. Continue to unpack meaning and connect ideas, chunk by chunk, using the guiding questions in the table below..

“It is a valiant and agonizing struggle | to focus. | not on what you have lost . . . but | on what you have been given.”

Sentence Chunk Discuss Meaning
It is a valiant and agonizing struggle -What is this sentence mostly about? (a struggle)
-What is a struggle? (a challenge, difficulty, or fight)
-What kind of struggle is it? (valiant and agonizing)
-What does valiant mean? Point out the Spanish cognate valiant for Spanish-speaking students. (brave, heroic)
-What does agonizing mean? (painful, difficult)
-What does it mean if a struggle is both valiant and agonizing? (It is difficult but heroic; it is worth the challenge.)
to focus -What does to focus mean? (pay attention to, concentrate on)
-What does the sentence say to focus on? (We don’t know yet; let’s look in the next chunks.)
on what you have been given -What does this chunk tell us about “focus”? (This is what to focus on: what you have been given.) -What kinds of “gifts” might the authors want people to focus on? (gifts such as love, kindness, faith, school, and support from friends and family)
not on what you have lost…but -What does this chunk tell us about “focus”? (This is what not to focus on: what you have lost.)
-Why might a refugee focus on what he or she has lost? (Refugees often lose so much: their homes, family members, friends, school, jobs, and toys and other belongings. It can be hard to focus on anything else.)
-How do not and but work together in this sentence? (They show a contrasting relationship between two ideas: not this, but that.)
-What ideas are being contrasted in this sentence? (Ideas about what to focus on: not what you have lost but what you have been given.)
-Why might this be a good message for everyone to focus on? (It helps us to appreciate what we have and not dwell on what we do not.)

After unlocking the key sentence, have students craft their own sentence using the following sentence frame:

It is a/an __________ struggle to focus not on __________ but on __________ .

Compare Pictures

Have students work with partners to discuss the pictures they drew earlier.

1. Have partners compare the drawings they each made. Ask partners to explain how their drawings show the ideas in the text on pages 6 and 7. As needed, have partners reread that part of the book aloud to each other.

2. Then have partners compare their drawings with Faizah’s drawing on page 18. What do they notice about the two drawings? What do they wonder about them?

3. Once students have completed their discussion, point out that on page 29, Faizah says that she can’t wait to show her picture to Mama. Have partners role play presenting their pictures to each other. One partner can pretend to be Faizah, and the other can be Mama.

4. Encourage the Faizahs to explain to Mama what the picture shows and why it is special. Encourage the Mamas to tell what they notice and like about the picture.

5. Then have students switch roles and repeat the role play.

Activity Support

Who Am I?

  • 1. Students write an acrostic poem. They use each letter in their names to write or draw something that tells something about them.
  • 2. Model an example. Write your name and then use the first few letters and write a few words or phrases that describe you.
  • 3. Work with the class to brainstorm words and phrases that students can use in their poems. Prompt students with categories, such as feelings, places, or things. You may also want to write the words in lists by beginning letter. Display the words so that students can refer to them when they write.
  • 4. Students write the letters in their names vertically on the left side of a piece of drawing paper. They write or draw a picture that begins with each letter and that tells something about them.

Activity Support

Perform a Skit

Partners write and perform a short skit that presents a constructive response to the bullies in the book.

  • Tell students to imagine they are on the playground and witness Asiya being bullied. How would they respond to the bullies and show support to Asiya?
  • Have partners work together to write a short, one-page script for their skit, rehearse it, and perform it for the class.
  • Students can use examples from the StopBullyingNow.gov video to spark ideas.

*Remind students that the skits should focus on constructive responses to bullying and should not include a reenactment of the bullying scene itself.

Activity Support

Author’s Craft: Personification

Have students work with partners to discuss the pictures they drew earlier.

1. Discuss students’ examples of non-human movie or TV characters with human-like qualities. Some suggestions: a rat that can talk, read recipes, and cook in a kitchen; a teakettle or candlestick holder that can sing. If possible, share online cartoons in which nonhuman objects or characters act like people.

2. Ask students to describe how the characters acted like humans. What human qualities did they have?

3. Remind students that in written stories, authors often describe nonhuman objects by giving them human qualities, such as laughing or speaking. Remind them that this is called personification.

4. Ask student pairs to identify an example of personification on the last two pages of the story. (30–31; Asiya’s hijab says “hello with a loud wave”; it says “I’ll always be here, like sisters.”)

5. Have partners discuss why this is an example of personification. (A hijab is an object, so it cannot talk. That is a human quality.)

6. Ask students to identify what idea or image the author wants to help readers picture. (It is as if the hijab is speaking to Faizah, welcoming her and reassuring her. It shows that Asiya’s hijab is special to both sisters.)

7. Now have students write a sentence that uses personification to describe Asiya’s hijab to show that it is special.
